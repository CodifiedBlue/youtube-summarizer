## Summary

Nate B Jones reviews Claude Fable 5 — a model he believes is roughly 10 trillion parameters and, for now, unavailable — arguing that its significance is not that it is *smarter* or better at benchmarks, but that it is *bigger*. It is the first model where the binding constraint stopped being the model's ability and became the user's ability to imagine an ask large enough to be worth its cost and power. Fable behaves like it expects to be checked (it quarantined bad data and built its own human review queue), which finally lets him hand off whole projects and walk away rather than hover.

The core argument is that three years of small, burned-once-so-ask-small habits have made every frontier model feel underwhelming in daily work, and Fable makes that gap impossible to ignore because small asks are simply a waste of its expense. The fix is a learnable skill Jones calls "detailed task imagination": finding the big, dirty, unowned jobs nobody has written down, assembling a rich data pack, defining what "done" looks like, handing it over, and reviewing the result like an owner reviewing a senior stakeholder. He closes on jobs: models like this kill only pure zero-judgment execution work, while everyone else becomes a "model manager" who directs, feeds, and judges the model — an invitation to career uplift rather than replacement.

## Key Takeaways

- Fable 5's leap is scale, not IQ — the new constraint is your imagination of the ask, not the model's capability.
- A "bigger model feeling" means you can hand off a whole job and actually walk away, because the model self-surfaces uncertain calls for human review instead of silently guessing.
- The model is not finished or perfect: it's expensive (~$50 per million output tokens), has weak visual/design taste, misses info in handwritten images unless forced, and every run still ends with review work on your desk.
- Years of "ask small, verify everything" habits — born from 2023–2024 hallucinations — are now out of date and are why AI has felt smaller than the headlines.
- The critical new skill is "detailed task imagination": give the model a whole job (source material + a clear definition of done + judgment guidelines), don't just prompt it a task.
- This is distinct from delegation — target the big, dirty, ambiguous jobs nobody has written down, not the named tasks already in your tracker.
- Fable is not a job killer except for pure zero-judgment execution work; these models need "model managers" to scope, feed, and review them, which is an opening for promotion and career growth.
